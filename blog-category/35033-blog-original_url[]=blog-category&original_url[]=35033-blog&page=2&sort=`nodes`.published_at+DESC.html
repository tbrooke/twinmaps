Reflections and Fish Tacos

Posted February 20, 2011 by Richard and Maxwell Brooke

 "Eat up" Jonathan's mother said. 

We looked at the food presented to us with eager eyes. We were at the home of Mr. and Ms. Campos, the parents of Jonathan, our driver. in Ensenada, Mexico. His mom had cooked fish tenders on a pan, and placed them beside tomatoes, lettuce, pico de gallo, tortillas, salsa, and lemon juice. Fish tacos: a classic meal from Ensenada, a Mexican coastal town. We had been on a long trip down to the city so the meal in front of us was welcomed by our hungry stomachs. We sat around the table with five others, Jonathan, his mother, father, sister, and Carlos, our guide. Spanish was their native language, so they spoke freely in Spanish. While we didn't understand Spanish, we appreciated the quiet silence of being removed from the conversation.

As we started our fish taco cadence, our minds wandered back to the events of the day. We had seen, and had yet to process, a lot.

Mid-Year Retreat: Gaining Brothers and Sisters from all walks of Life

Posted February 13, 2011 by Richard and Maxwell Brooke

 "Sometimes, the most urgent thing you can do is take a complete rest." Ashleigh Brilliant

If you read our last blog closely, you noticed that we were gone from Wednesday until Sunday (February 9th until the 13th) on the Mid-Year Volunteer Retreat for the Partners in Service Volunteers. We traveled by plane to San Antonio TX, and then took a 40 minute drive to Sunset Falls Retreat center in New Braufells, TX. After a delay in Phoenix, changing a connecting flight in Dallas-Fort Worth, and finding ways to waste time on a 3 hour layover, we finally arrived in San Antonio. We were picked up at the airport and shared the ride out to the retreat center with David, a Ukrainian volunteer from Hopewell Farms. David was the first volunteer that we met on this trip at Hopewell Farms so it was nice to catch up with him and see how much he had grown over his time in the states.  We arrived at the retreat center at 9:00 Central Time. 

The retreat's theme concerned the Sabbath and how to honor it. And this theme could not come at a better time. We had our discussions lead by Reverend Daniel Moser and we also participated by sharing about our volunteer experiences at our sites. We revisited many of the themes that we've covered already: we discussed how homelessness can be overcome with help, how mental illness can be a spring board for success, how disaster victims need our immediate help, and how border issues are among the most tragic stories we've heard. While we discussed our projects, we each gained a glimpse into the work that all of the volunteers are doing across the United States.

Gould Farm Feature

Posted January 27, 2011 by Richard and Maxwell Brooke

  "You will never do anything in this world without courage. It is the greatest quality of the mind next to honor." - Aristotle

The Gould Farm Feature is posted!

Gould Farm is a residential treatment program for adults with mental illness. Individuals with any type of illness can come in and be a part of a work program that highlights life skills, clinical recovery and intentional community. The goal of each guest is to recover in all three of these facets of their development. With a dedicated staff and volunteer group, Gould Farm has been successful for nearly 100 years. 

The first thing you notice when you spend sometime at Gould Farm is that it's very hard to distinguish the difference between the guests, staff, and volunteers. The only reason we knew the volunteers is because we were introduced to them because that's who we were going to cover that week. For the first couple of days we asked people who was who and if someone was a staff or a guest. We then realized that it was better to not ask and just not ever know who was who.

A Day In Mexico: Part 1

Posted January 19, 2011 by Richard and Maxwell Brooke

 "Americans have a problem with migrants. They want them to do the menial jobs, but don't want them living in the community." - Kathy Freudenberg

Southern Arizonians can tell you a lot about their state. They can tell you about it's history, it's politics, it's issues, and it's future. They are an intelligent bunch and each have their personal stories about how they got there, what they're doing, their community activities and how much they love the weather. But, their is one issue in southern Arizona that is at the focus of their policies, their activism, and their daily lives: Immigration. 

When we filming in Sahuarita on Friday, we met with Shura, a short, spunky, grey-haired lady with the energy of a teenager and the personality of a martyr. Shura told us that she takes a weekly trip across the border into Mexico. While there, she delivers goods to the needy such as blankets, food, and basic toiletries. She told us that we were welcome to come along with her on a trip the morning of January 18th. 

We woke up that morning and met Shura, and many other US citizens in Green Valley, AZ, which is about 30 miles north of Nogales, the first town in Mexico when you cross the border. We drove to the last gas station on Interstate 19, put all of the goods-to-be-delivered in one vehicle, parked the car, grabbed our cameras, and started our walk to customs.
