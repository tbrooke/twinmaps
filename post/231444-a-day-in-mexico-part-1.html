A Day In Mexico: Part 1

Posted January 19, 2011 04:52 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     2 comments

"Americans have a problem with migrants. They want them to do the menial jobs, but don't want them living in the community." - Kathy Freudenberg
 
Southern Arizonians can tell you a lot about their state. They can tell you about it's history, it's politics, it's issues, and it's future. They are an intelligent bunch and each has their personal stories about how they got there, what they're doing, their community activities and how much they love the weather. But, their is one issue in southern Arizona that is at the focus of their policies, their activism, and their daily lives: Immigration. 
 
When we filmed in Sahuarita on Friday, we met with Shura, a short, spunky, grey-haired lady with the energy of a teenager and the personality of a martyr. Shura told us that she takes a weekly trip across the border into Mexico. While there, she delivers goods to the needy such as blankets, food, and basic toiletries. She told us that we were welcome to come along with her on a trip the morning of January 18th. 
 
We woke up that morning and met Shura, and many other US citizens in Green Valley, AZ, which is about 30 miles north of Nogales, the first town in Mexico when you cross the border. We drove to the last gas station on Interstate 19, put all of the goods-to-be-delivered in one vehicle, parked the car, grabbed our cameras, and started our walk to customs. 
 
When we arrived at the customs gate to go into Mexico, there were no agents there to stop us. There were agents at customs, but they were not concerned with pedestrians entering Mexico. Their concern was rather focused on people/cargo/vehicles coming from Nogales, Mexico into the US. The traffic in was four lanes long with agents at every lane. Trucks, passengers, workers, and travelers all had to wait hours to have their identification checked and screened while we walked right in without anyone speaking a word to us. 
 
But the moment you cross over into Mexico, you know why no one questions why you are going to Mexico. The better question is why one would want to go into Mexico. Nogales is a border city, and has become an epicenter for migrants not just from Mexico, but from all over Central America. If you walk the streets of Nogales, your ears will be filled with the sounds of different dialects from all over the continent. People leave their home countries for a myriad of reasons: fear of safety, lack of economic prosperity, family lives in the US... anything. While there are many permanent residents of Nogales, the migrants that risk their lives, give up their money, and leave behind their past life because they are not happy with their current conditions. They want to do something about it. They are not satisfied with the status quo. 
 
When we cross over, we walk another half mile to a place called the "Comedor," which serves breakfast to anyone. It is run by the Jesuits who knows Shura and the others we were traveling with. 
It should be noted at this time that Shura is treated like a saint in Nogales. She walks the streets and everyone knows who she is. They flock to her, hold her hand, and walk with her to wherever it is that she is going. She goes to the Comedor, everyone follows. 
 
We get our gear out and immediately start to record. 
 
We get there, and the line to enter for a meal is huge. There are people from all over the city who have come to just get a meal. While in line, two women are sitting on the curb with what look like injuries to their legs. A man who is versed in First Aid and has a medicine kit is assisting them. Our packages that we had delivered from the US arrive safely and with no incident. We start to pass out blankets to everyone. They don't look that comfortable, but they will last for the rest of the cold desert nights. 
 
Commotion from inside. We turn to see Shura enter and are told to continue to pass out blankets. A few minutes later, a truck arrives. Men from inside assist an old man from inside out to the truck. We find out later that he had an episode which required medical attention. 
 
Another group of Americans, who arrived earlier, are busy transporting goods and people. They are Unitarian Universalists. They see the cameras and tell us that we need to capture everything. They tell us that this story must be told. 
 
We start a conversation with the man who was giving first aid to the women, Pancho. He is fluent in English. We learn why: he had been living in Phoenix with this wife and children for 30 years before he was deported back to Mexico in 2008. Once he was back in Mexico, he decided that he would reapply for papers back into the US, a long and strenuous process that will take many years. At the present time, gives basic first aid to anyone on the streets of Nogales so long as he can help them. 
 
"If God will provide, I will help anyone who needs it." 
 
We are finally allowed access into the Comedor. Breakfast is over and some individuals are still speaking with Shura. She looks over to us and asks if we want to stay with her. We feel safe with her and know that we will see the real side of Nogales if we follow her. We decide to be with her wherever she is. She, two others from the group, and a group of three migrants are in our group. We leave the Comedor and walk further into Nogales. 
 
As we continue walking, we start to speak with a 19 year old named Miguel. Miguel is also fluent in English. He tells that he was born in Mexico but had lived in California from the time he was little. He grew up in California and was eventually deported from California when he was 16. He is upset with his current conditions and plans on doing something about it. He is following Shura. 
 
We press on and eventually reach a branch of the Mexican Immigration Office. After Shura speaks with people inside, we drop one of the men off and go back the way we came. 
 
We stop at bus garage on the way back. The second man of our group has now become very important to Shura and the conversation she is having with the owner. They continue to speak with a translator and eventually come to an agreement. We leave the man there and start our walk back to Comedor. 
 
As we walk back, Miguel and Shura are talking tightly. They are moving quickly and keeping their conversation to themselves. Shura's body language says that she's concerned and Miguel's says that he is stubborn. Eventually, we hear that Miguel wants to cross the border on Friday. He wants to make it back to California. Shura is telling him that it is too treacherous to go alone and that he will get caught if he goes. She says that if the US government doesn't catch him, whoever will would kill him. Miguel protests but Shura makes her argument clear. 
 
We return to Comedor with Miguel and Shura has resigned. Miguel fully intends to cross the border. Defeated, Shura gives a few more dollar bills to Miguel.
 
We wish him the best of luck and depart, starting our trek back to customs. 
 
While walking back to customs, we schedule a meeting with Shura and tell her that the experience we had this morning was life-altering. 
 
We reach customs. The agent in charge of pedestrians calls us forward individually. He asks for our passport and our activities while in Mexico. He stares with a concerned look at the camera in our hands. He tells us to move forward. 
 
Immediately afterwards, we try to do a stand-up to capture what we just experienced. In the middle of it, we are told that we cannot film at customs.
 
We turn our cameras off and move along.
