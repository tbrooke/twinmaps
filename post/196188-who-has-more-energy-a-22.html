Who has more energy, a 22 year old or a 73 year old?

Posted December 5, 2010 10:31 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     2 comments

"The only thing money gives you is the freedom of not worrying about money." - Johnny Carson

Let's play a game. 

We know you want to play a game with us. You're sitting at your desk/laptop checking your email/facebook/news and avoiding work/homework/studying. We get it. We know because we do it all the time. 

So play with us. 

Imagine for a second, a volunteer. 

Just a regular volunteer. 

Someone who has decided to give up their time/efforts/money and work at an agency for the betterment of others. This person has a multitude of talents. They are usually smart, well-educated, and aware. But what separates them from most people is their empathy. They love to help, they love other people, and they love what they do. 

What are you imagining? 

If you're like most people, you are imagining a twenty-something, fresh-out-of-college, greenhorn. This is what many people think of as a "volunteer." 

So imagine our surprise when we roll up to Abernethy Laurels Retirement Community and are greeted by the volunteers there. 

They are a 70+ year old couple. 

Bill and Pat Lincoln have been married for over 50 years. They have been teachers, principals, community organizers, business owners, and parents. They lived their lives in western Massachusetts like most normal couples, and were happy because of it. 

But, one day, they said to themselves: "What else could we do to help others? How can we progress the world around us?"

"What about people who need our help?"

For the past few years, they have been volunteering all across the United States (North Carolina, Arizona, Massachusetts) at sites where they felt like they could serve. They have volunteered the most time at Abernethy Laurels Retirement Center and have served their peers for nearly two years now. They feel a strong connection to the individuals they serve while there and enjoy every facet of their work. As a couple, they have the ability to reach people that others might not be able to.

And their energy, even in their seventies, is at a level that makes twenty-two year olds jealous. They get up at 5:00 am every morning to make sure they are available for every event at ALRC. They participate in EVERYTHING. They call out bingo numbers, mark off laundry, help sing songs, facilitate events, set up a reception, and do a multitude of other tasks. While doing this, they are constantly socializing with the residents. They make them feel comfortable and are constantly looking for ways to improve others around them. It's funny to see them do this because while the guests are sitting down and receiving care, Bill and Pat, their peers, are out of their seats, running around, and multitasking their work. In their own words, they do what ever they are needed to do. 

Because of their "retirement job" they have found a reinvigorated meaning. They have met many people throughout their time as volunteers, some old, some young, some well-to-do, some with nothing, but their story has remained the same: "Go where we feel we are most needed." 

And they've done just that. 

And without knowing it, they were needed to help refocus us on our travels. 

That's the sign of a good volunteer. Not how old they are, or how educated, or how empathetic. Sure, a good volunteer is all of those things and does what they need to do when the time comes. 

But.

The sign of an incredible volunteer is being where they are needed when they are not expected to be. 

That's Bill and Pat for you.
