Mid-Year Retreat: Gaining Brothers and Sisters from all walks of Life

Posted February 13, 2011 09:39 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     1 comments

"Sometimes, the most urgent thing you can do is take a complete rest." Ashleigh Brilliant

If you read our last blog closely, you noticed that we were gone from Wednesday until Sunday (February 9th until the 13th) on the Mid-Year Volunteer Retreat for the Partners in Service Volunteers. We traveled by plane to San Antonio TX, and then took a 40 minute drive to Sunset Falls Retreat center in New Braufells, TX. After a delay in Phoenix, changing a connecting flight in Dallas-Fort Worth, and finding ways to waste time on a 3 hour layover, we finally arrived in San Antonio. We were picked up at the airport and shared the ride out to the retreat center with David, a Ukrainian volunteer from Hopewell Farms. David was the first volunteer that we met on this trip at Hopewell Farms so it was nice to catch up with him and see how much he had grown over his time in the states.  We arrived at the retreat center at 9:00 Central Time. 

The retreat's theme concerned the Sabbath and how to honor it. And this theme could not come at a better time. We had our discussions lead by Reverend Daniel Moser and we also participated by sharing about our volunteer experiences at our sites. We revisited many of the themes that we've covered already: we discussed how homelessness can be overcome with help, how mental illness can be a spring board for success, how disaster victims need our immediate help, and how border issues are among the most tragic stories we've heard. While we discussed our projects, we each gained a glimpse into the work that all of the volunteers are doing across the United States. 

The volunteers themselves are equally as incredible. The majority of them (nearly 3/4ths) are German citizens who are volunteering abroad for one year (Two others were Belgian and one other was Ukrainian). While it was different to hear another language besides English, we knew we were in the presence of good people due to their sincerity. Each of the foreign volunteers brought kindness, trust, and hope into the retreat as we grew our friendships beyond that of just service. As the week progressed, we became brothers and sisters with each other. We talked about everything from our futures, politics, and sports. We played games, shared meals, swapped rooms... everything that camp should be we did it. 

So, for lack of time, and focus, (we have a drive to San Diego tomorrow), let's just give you the highlights of the trip in note form:

We saw many volunteers at the retreat that we had already seen on this trip (Kai, Kevin, and Robin from St. Louis; Nelson from Lancaster, Yannick from Massachusetts), met volunteers that we would meet (Keith in Portland, Hannah in Seattle, Josef and Hanna in Iowa) and met others that we wouldn't have had the chance to meet otherwise (Niklaus, Wadim, Andre, and many many others). 

We ran three times during the retreat. We had to exercise a little bit during camp. We don't say this to feel good about ourselves, we say it because we were beat every time by our fellow runners. 

Everyone in Germany has played this game and is highly skilled at it. 

When you get right down to it, there isn't much difference between a 20 something from Europe and a 20 something from America. 

We visited the Alamo in San Antonio. Great chance to see a part of Texas and US History. 

We ate very well on this retreat. We had French Toast in the morning, delicious camp lunches and dinners, and oranges and apples for lecture snacks. It was awesome. 

We had an exercise where we walked around the campsite for 30 minutes in complete silence. While the exercise was fulfilling, Max found himself playing a game on large rocks. No one is surprised. 

This picture makes all of us laugh to no end. 

To everyone on the retreat: Thank you for making it one of the best few days of this trip. We came as twins, and left with more brothers and sisters than we know what to do with. We'll see you all in the future, whether it be in the states or in Europe. But we promise we'll see you all again.
