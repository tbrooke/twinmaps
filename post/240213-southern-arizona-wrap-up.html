Southern Arizona Wrap Up

Posted January 30, 2011 09:57 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     2 comments

 

"By three methods may we learn wisdom: first, by reflection, which is noblest; second, by imitation, which is easiest; and third, by experience, which is most bitter." - Confucius 

While we were traveling across the country to Arizona(during the week of January 11th, which seems like an eternity ago), we had a brief conversation. We knew that we would be covering Southern Arizona's most pressing issue: immigration. We spoke about it from a policy standpoint: how do we resolve the problem we now face? Being from North Carolina, we only feel the indirect effects of the issue, so we had to really change our perspective to understand what people in Southern Arizona would be feeling. There is the issue of migrants, illegal crossings, and the construction of the wall. How should we as a nation advance in an issue that is so volatile and divisive on the subject? 

But that's how we spoke about it: objective policy. 

However, as we've grown into our homes in Southern Arizona (one week in Sahuarita and the other in Tucson), we've seen some incredible things. An activist community has been a bright spot in the raw and dry desert and the young volunteers have given us a community that we have deeply missed on our travels. But when they speak about Border Issues, they don't talk about it from a policy standpoint. Unless you talk about specifics, policy isn't the problem. 

It's not a political issue; it's a humanitarian one. 

People are dying. Plain and simple. They die trying to cross the border and they die because they don't have enough food. Last year alone there were over 200 documented deaths in the deserts of Arizona. From 1999-2009, there were over 1700 documented deaths in the deserts of Arizona. These numbers do not include the three other states that share a border with Mexico and their deaths of border crossers: California, New Mexico and Texas. To most of the country, Border Issues are not a problem to them because they do not have to see or feel the effects of it; however, this issue is real and so are the effects of it. 

Lucky for Southern Arizona, the community there is set on reducing the number of deaths. We've met people that sacrifice their lives, earnings, and talents to help others that live in poor conditions in another country. We've also met some of the most talented, driven, and ambitious young volunteers throughout this trip. Both parties opened up their communities, homes and lives to us so that we could capture the issues here. To them, the political issues surrounding immigration are superseded by the humanitarian issues around it. 

We cannot thank everyone enough for making our time in Southern Arizona incredible. We met activists that were ensuring their efforts were helping others, we crossed the border twice in Nogales, Mexico, and we made friendships with young volunteers that we will carry into our lifetime. 

And when you deal with this issue, a caring friend is all you need to make sure you get by. 

Other things: 

We're in Phoenix, AZ to cover another Volunteer Corps c/o the Church of the Beatitudes. Four lovely young ladies. They are great hosts... This is a good omen. 

At our previous location, BorderLinks, one of the Young Adults Volunteers there blogged about us. Goooo publicity! The author, Stevie, has a nice passage that sums up our time in Tucson pretty well. Her words, incredibly enough, apply to us also. Read it below and be sure to follow her on her journey. 

I know right now I'm glad to have this experience. I meet so many people that, after the small talk questions of "what do you do?" and find out what I'm doing in Tucson this year respond with something along the lines of "Oh, I wish I could do something like that." And I could- I wasn't turning down anything that wouldn't still be available to me after this year. I'll make ends meet for now, and return to a steady paycheck and a normal routine someday. But right now, my eyes are more opened to the world around me. The ills and beauty of people, nature, and policy have come alive to me in the desert here, and I'm happy to be here to have it bring me life.
