The Top Ten Things We've Learned Since Starting Twin Maps

Posted April 4, 2011 10:21 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     2 comments

"We may encounter many defeats, but we must not be defeated." Maya Angelou

Blogging to you live, from White Swan, Washington, Twin Maps is back on the road!

For the past two weeks, we've been on break. We wrapped up filming in Seattle and started our break by flying back home to North Carolina. We left our car with family in Seattle so the Kia could keep some miles off of her, and when we arrived back home, we were happily surrounded with family, friends, and love. We caught up with old acquaintances, saw some life-long friends, and made time for our closest friends and family in a (very) quick two weeks. It's over now and we're back on the road and have a busy few weeks ahead of us. We're currently on assignment in White Swan, WA covering the Yakama Christian Mission. Over the next 4 weeks, will be in South Dakota, Nebraska, Iowa, Illinois, Kentucky, and Mississippi. It's going to be a very busy month ahead of us.

But before we dive into our third and final leg of our trip, we want to share with you one of our favorite moments from our time back home. We were scheduled to give a presentation at our church, Mt. Zion UCC in China Grove, NC, for Sunday School. However, our pastor woke up sick the same morning and could not make it to church. Our church leadership has handled a situation like this with much grace before, so it was a surprise when they asked us if we could deliver the message. We accepted, and decided that we would take a portion of our presentation from earlier that morning and dive into more detail. We talked about the Top Ten Things that we have learned while traveling with each other.

We wanted to recreate this list for our readers here.

10. Gas Prices are expensive... Everywhere!

It doesn't matter where you go, who you talk to, what part of the country you're in, gas prices are devilishly high. They affect everyone, everywhere and everyone complains about them.

9. When you give, you receive more.

This was a lesson that we learned very early in our travels. Wherever we traveled, we met a group of selfless, committed people who gave their talents, intelligence, ambitions and fortunes to the betterment of society and their neighbors. When asked why they did things for their community, the answer was always the same: "Whenever we help others, we help ourselves. What we get out of helping others is greater than the help we can give to others." Volunteers have this attitude wherever we go. It is thankless and special.

8. It is a challenge to do professional work with your brother.

And it's even worse when you're twins. We didn't go to the same University, so after four years of being apart, we had to (quickly) learn how to work together again. It wasn't without its speedbumps, but we think we got the hang of it now.

7. The next story is always different from the last.

On this trip, we have visited countless homeless shelters, transitional living centers, volunteer corps, and services to the poor. You would think that eventually these places would have the same stories over and over again; eventually, we'd fall a cycle of monotony when covering another shelter. But that's the exact opposite of what we've found. Once you get beyond the statistics and actually get to know the people you're covering, you find unique personality and people no matter where we are. We get to approach each story with fresh eyes every time, and it's because we work with some of the strongest and bravest people we have ever met.

6. If you ever want us to do anything for you, just bribe us with food.

It's kind of like college.

5. There is always a need.

Whenever a person says that they "want to make a difference," some thoughts immediately turn to international need. Who can blame them? Africa is a war-torn continent, the Middle-East is in turmoil, and disaster recovery is needed every time a catastrophe occurs. But, as we have found out, the most immediate and influential impact a person can make is in their community. You can find a need within a five mile radius of your home, and you will grow and advance your community in ways you may have thought impossible. Get out there, find a problem, volunteer, and make a difference.

4. The maturity level of a 23 year old American and a 20 year old German are the exact same.

While traveling, we have had the pleasure of living/working/spending time with Germans who are volunteering in the United States. We have more in common with them than we thought, as we talk, think, act, and do the same things that they do. We're glad that we have gained brothers and sisters from across borders on this trip.

3. Always be in the moment.

This is one that is bittersweet for us. Whenever we travel, we know that our time is limited and fleeting with those around us. We have to always make sure that we leave a good impression, but we also want to make a real connection with people. We need interaction, growth, and connection with the people around us. Every single thing you do with a person is important: every conversation, every action, every nuance. Because you don't know how long or how short your time is with a person, and that moment could be the last time you ever see that person. You never know what type of impression someone will make on your lifetime.

2. Two guys traveling/living in a car for an entire year makes for a smelly car.

Not much more needs to be said about that one.

1. There is so much good in this world.

Whenever you turn on the news, you hear a story about how the world is suffering. Poverty is on the rise, crime is up, politicians are corrupt and your neighborhood isn't safe anymore. There isn't much hope for a world whose people are not inherently good.

While on this trip, we've seen the exact opposite. We've seen the good in people shine through the tough times that are ahead of them. We've seen people overcome struggles to attain a better life. We've seen people sacrifice their talents, time, and fortune just to give their neighbor a hand. There are good people in this world, and they want to be agents of change.

This project is meant to highlight volunteer work throughout the United States; we want to capture ordinary people actively changing their communities around them. At a deeper level, however, we want to see people for how they really are. And in our travels, we have met people that want to experience the world in a real and raw way. They want to see people as they are, not how society paints them. They want to experience God in an undiluted way.

For every bit of evil in this world, there is good. For every inkling of fear, there is courage and kindness. For every bit of hate, there is love.

We can only hope that our work reflects what we have seen. At the end of it all, if we only show reflections of love, then Twin Maps has been successful.
