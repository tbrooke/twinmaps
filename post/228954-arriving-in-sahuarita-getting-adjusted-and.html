Arriving in Sahuarita, Getting adjusted, and back to WORK!

Posted January 16, 2011 01:14 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke    

"The love of one's country is a splendid thing. But why should love stop at the border."

-Pablo Casias

Let's bring you up to speed, shall we? 

We woke up from our hotel in Fort Smith, Arkansas and decided now it would be a good time to get our oil changed. We found the nearest Wal-Mart, put our car in the shop, and bought more travel items for our trip (screen cleaners, jump ropes, etc.) Never again will we waste time in a Wal-Mart for utter fear of blowing more money. 

So we left Fort Smith and started our trek across Oklahoma and Texas. For the record, there is little to see on Interstate - 40 in Oklahoma and Texas. All you see is this: 

We stopped for dinner in Amarillo, TX for some Tex-Mex at a restaurant called "Jorge's". It was awesome. 

After eating, we left for our accommodations in Friona, Texas. Friona is a small town with about 4000 people in it, so it reminded us of China Grove. Our hosts, the Renners, were kind and generous. Ms. Renner even made us breakfast in the morning. Which we needed, because we would be starting our trek across West Texas towards Arizona. 

Let us tell you something. Have you ever traveled through West Texas? How about New Mexico? Well, if you'd like to emulate that, we have a plan: Go find the flatest road you can find. If there are any trees in your windows, put up hand-drawn pictures of flat land and a mountain in the background and put them up in your windows. Turn up your heat in the car to 85 degrees. Hire someone to create tumbleweeds and roll them around your car. Drive. When you reach the end of the flat road, turn around and go back the way you came. Every 40 minutes to an hour, have a gas station and a McDonalds pop up. Do this for 8 hours. If you haven't pulled over, got out and started chasing after desert mirages, Congratulations. You have survived a trip through West Texas and New Mexico. 

We arrived in Sahuarita, Arizona around 6:30 Mountain Time, an hour earlier than anticipated. We had new equipment (Shoulder mount!!!) delivered to Good Shepherd UCC which was where we were meeting our hosts, Ann and Bill Cochran. We got our equipment, but they had choir practice at 7:00 until 8:30. They recommended a Mexican Restaurant down the road from the church, which we took them up on. After their choir practice was over, we traveled to their home in Green Valley, AZ. 

And here's where we had trouble. 

The Cochrans have cats. 

Three of them. 

We are allergic to cats. Max, is deathly allergic. 

We were upset that we couldn't stay with them, but the Cochrans had a plan: they would call a friend, Martha Belle, who didn't have cats and could accommodate us for space. We left for Martha's home immediately and introduced ourselves. She is like most everyone we've seen on this trip: kind, generous, and accommodating. We spoke a little bit longer and enjoyed each other's company a bit more before retiring for the night. We slept more soundly than we had in days, our travels finally over. 

We are in Sahuarita for two reasons: We are covering the Border Fair and a Young Adult Service Community (YASC) here. The Border Fair is an annual conference that discusses and educates about immigration, border crossers, and reform. Being a few miles from the US-Mexico border, Sahuarita and the Tucson area are big hubs for immigration issues. They are directly affected by any federal reform or state immigration law, such as SB 1040. The Reverend at Good Shepherd UCC, Randy Mayer, holds the Border Fair every year at the church itself due to its location and work with local agencies. On Friday night, they have a concert and on Saturday, they have the big day for the seminar. The YASC group is a group similar to the Westmoreland Volunteer Corps, as the YASC group was modeled directly after Westmoreland. There is a YASC in both Sahuarita and Phoenix, both of which we will highlight. 

Friday and Saturday were very long days for us. 

Friday, we woke up and went over the Good Shepherd UCC to introduce ourselves to both Reverend Mayer and the talent for the concert tonight, Guy Davis, a blues musician from New York City. After discussions about filming and set up, we went back over to the Cochrans who wanted to treat us to a homemade breakfast for lunch. Biscuits and gravy, sausage, and fruit: truly a great meal. After the meal, we ran up to Tucson to do two things: to rig up our shoulder brace and to meet with Bob and Linda Maddox. 

If you have a good memory, you know that Bob and Linda Maddox were our hosts while we were in DC. They were actually the ones that suggested we go to the Border Fair in January while we were in DC. So they were the reason that we were in Arizona to begin with. We saw them briefly and promised to see them tonight at the show. After that quick reunion, we spent the rest of our afternoon in Lowe's Home Improvement trying to figure out a counter-balance for the new shoulder brace. We have something that (kinda) works now. 

We returned to Good Shepherd UCC at 6:15 pm for a 7:00 pm show and filmed nearly the whole thing. Guy Davis in an incredible talent. If you are a fan of bluegrass, country, or the blues at all, you need to listen to him. Now. 

While filming, we had the chance to introduce ourselves to both the Sahuarita and Phoenix YASC groups. They were both in Sahuarita for the Border Fair. There are eight young ladies in both groups (four in each). We are excited to work with them and get to know their stories better. 

The show wrapped up at 10:00 pm, we packed up, left, and passed out at Martha's, knowing that the next day would be just a long. 

We woke up and again went to Good Shepherd UCC for the conference portion of the Border Fair. 

Immigration reform is one of the largest issues in Arizona and for good reason. They share a border with Mexico and because of that, they are directly affected by immigrants. The amount of information involved with this issue is astronomical, as there were doctors, lawyers, forensic scientists, and naturalized citizens that offered their knowledge to all the participants at the Border Fair. The lectures were moving, as we heard stories about immigrants crossing the desert for a better life, disturbing, as we heard a doctor recount the number of skeletons they uncover every year, and informative, as we learned about new legislation coming through Arizona's state legislature. 

The Border Fair wrapped up at 2:30 pm. Perfect timing to make it to a restaurant to watch football and eat dinner. 

 

Other notes: 

After our last blog, we received the following message from one of our favorite readers (Mom), via email. 

Honey, you look so tired.  I know it has been a hard day.  I hope you get a good night's sleep and wake up energized in the morning.  Sometimes, I feel just like you do, but tomorrow is always another day!  

 

We realize how tired we look. But we aren't too concerned. We'll be okay. Thanks Mom! We love you too!

It was ironic to be stuck in "SNOWMAGEDON" in Nashville, TN and then wake up everyday to sunny, 70 degree weather in Tucson, AZ. It is so wonderful, we feel like we've started summer 4 months early.

 

Tech Problems: As soon as we got out here to Good Shepherd UCC, we put together our new "toys" and rigged our new shoulder mount.  We have 20 pounds of washers on the back of our shoulder mount for counter weight.  We were both a little worried that the way we rigged it might not be stable enough to hold it but we soon found out that the rig works. Only problem of the whole weekend with the new rig was on the first time we filmed with the new rig Max put the rig on a table and turned his back for one second. BOOOM! That was the sound of all the weights crashing onto the ground and a look of total embarrassment on Max's face. It also messed up one of our main lenses so now it doesn't keep the same focus when we zoom out. Not good.

Richard's computer is giving him the White Screen of Death(WSOD) and is now using Max's Mac so we can no longer do work separately at the time being. 

Don't worry, we'll fix it.
