Shanty Towns, Smoke, and Toxic Water

Posted February 16, 2011 11:18 PM by Richard and Maxwell Brooke     3 comments

"In Mexico, you have death very close. That's true for all human beings because it's a part of life, but in Mexico, death can be found in many things." Gael Garcia Bernal

The first thing you notice is the smell. 

When you step out of your vehicle and lay your eyes on the shanty town neighborhood of Chilpancingo in Tijuana, the first thing you notice is the smell. 

It is toxic. 

And it is coming from their water.

Once you recover from the rancid smell of the neighborhood's water source, you look around and discover that the neighborhood of Chilpancingo can hardly be called a neighborhood at all. It's more like a collection of wooden walls that people sleep in. These homes stretch on for miles. 

"Look over there!" Carlos Correa, our guide and program director for Centro Romero, points. "Someone is burning something." He says with disdain. 

"What do you think they are burning?" Jonathan, our 22 year old driver, asks. 

"Tires, chemicals, waste..." Carlos says with resignation. "Whatever it is, it's toxic." 

 

"This is nothing new." Carlos continues. "Most of these people work in the factory right up the road. If the factory doesn't want to get rid of the waste, they pass it on to their employees to destroy or use them. This is a weekly occurrence." 

This is how some people in Tijuana live. They live in extreme conditions of poverty, their homes are deteriorated, their health is poor, and their children have little chance of a better life. But this is how life is in Tijuana, the border city that is at the northern and western most point of Mexico, sharing a border with California. It is an epicenter of poverty and corruption. For Carlos, however, this is where his mission lies. 

Centro Romero is an organization that facilitates educational-transformational immersion seminars along the border focusing on issues of globalization, economic policies, immigration, and community empowerment. One of the services that Centro Romero provides is an immersion experience for Americans that want to see how people live on the other side of the border. Participants get to see for themselves the lifestyle that many Mexicans live in. No spin, no policy, just a raw in-your-face experience.

We're going to experience a part of that immersion. 

Tomorrow, we will be driving back into Mexico. We will be staying in the night in Ensenada, Mexico. It's south of Tijuana and Jonathan's mother lives there. After lots of filming, we'll head back to Tijuana and then return to the US. You shouldn't expect an update from us until we get back to the US on Friday. 

 

Once we felt as if we had enough footage, we crossed back over the bridge leading into Chilpancingo. The water that was previously black was now brown. Bubbles had started forming where the water was stagnant, a sign of a chemical reaction.

As we pressed on, we noticed a small girl. She was with her family, but she had found something to distract her while adults chatted. She walked to the river and started to pick up a few rocks on the way. She would need this for her own ammunition. She stopped, set her body, and started to throw rocks into the toxic river. 

Keep fighting, kid.
